--- Raw model answer ---
It's a judgment call that comes down to a few signals we look at in parallel: error rate, latency, and whether the blast radius is still growing. If those three are all trending the wrong way in the first 10 to 15 minutes, rollback wins every time. Fix-forward only makes sense when the regression is narrow, well-understood, and we already have a candidate patch. In that incident, write-path errors were climbing across two regions, latency was degrading for new writes, and customer impact was spreading, so rollback was the obvious move.

A concrete next step is to decide upfront who has rollback authority and what the triggering thresholds are, so you don't relitigate the decision during the incident.
