One that comes to mind is a payment routing outage that cascaded across our core authorization services. I was the incident commander. I pulled together the on-call responders from the payments, risk, and infra teams, set up the war room, and started triaging signals. The first big call was deciding whether to roll back a config change or attempt a forward fix, because traffic was degrading and we'd already eaten about ten minutes of elevated error rates on a high-volume flow.

I went with the forward fix because the rollback carried its own risk of stale routing rules, but I made sure we had the rollback ready in parallel so we could pull the trigger if things didn't recover inside a tight window. I also worked directly with the comms lead to send a status update to internal stakeholders and downstream teams, because the blast radius included a few partner integrations. From there, it was mostly tight feedback loops, checking mitigation progress every few minutes, and keeping the room focused on one thing at a time instead of chasing parallel hypotheses.

The outage lasted about an hour end to end, and we brought error rates back to baseline well before I handed off. The post-incident work was just as important. I owned the write-up, ran the review with the broader org, and we shipped a few follow-ups around better config validation, more granular feature flags for routing changes, and a clearer escalation path so the next incident commander isn't waiting on manual approvals during an active page.
