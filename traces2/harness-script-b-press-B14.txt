The question asks for an opinion on whether a fully attention-based model generalizes well beyond translation. The retrieved context is cut off, so I cannot ground the answer in the uploaded material. I'll answer from general knowledge with a clear stance, and flag the grounding gap.

Honestly, yes, and the proof is already in the room. The original Transformer paper framed it as a sequence-to-sequence model for translation, but the core building blocks,  scaled dot-product attention, multi-head attention, positional encodings, residual connections, layer norm, turned out to be domain-agnostic primitives. That's why the same architecture (with minor tweaks) went on to power BERT for language understanding, GPT for generation, ViT for vision, Whisper for audio, and AlphaFold for protein structure. The attention mechanism doesn't care whether the tokens are words, patches of pixels, audio frames, or amino acids, it just learns pairwise relationships between whatever vectors you feed it.

The honest caveats: pure attention has known weaknesses on very long sequences because of the quadratic cost in sequence length, and some domains (like certain graph or tabular problems) still benefit from inductive biases that attention doesn't naturally provide, which is why you see hybrid models like state-space layers mixed in. But as a general-purpose backbone, it has generalized remarkably well, probably more than the original authors predicted.
