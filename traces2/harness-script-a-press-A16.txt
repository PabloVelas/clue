--- Raw model answer ---
"Honestly, one of the harder disagreement calls I made was when I owned the reconciliation system rebuild at Stripe. The batch job had been running on Hadoop for years, every team had muscle memory around it, and the finance org was deeply attached to the daily numbers it produced. A few senior engineers and a director wanted to keep extending it with incremental patches because the risk felt lower. I disagreed. I was convinced that the real problem was architectural, not operational, so no amount of tuning would actually move the latency or the cost.

What I did was write up an RFC that laid out the current trajectory in numbers, the failure modes that were getting more likely as volume grew, and a proposed streaming design on Kafka and Flink. Then I pushed for a real design review instead of letting it get decided in side channels. There were a couple of tense sessions, and I had to be willing to be wrong in public, which I was, on one of the consistency trade-offs. We revised the design around it.

The system we shipped now processes about 4.2 billion ledger entries a day. End-to-end reconciliation latency came down from 14 hours to 38 minutes at p99, and we cut roughly 2.3 million a year in compute. The director who pushed back ended up sponsoring the rollout, which I think was the best part of it."
